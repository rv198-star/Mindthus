## Scope

This is not a universal definition of all King Hu films. It is a scoped profile for
cinematic prompt and storyboard image generation when the target is a King Hu wuxia
grammar: patient suspense, spatial intelligence, Beijing opera rhythm, dance-like
combat, inn / confined-space faction choreography, moral and political pressure,
female knight-errant presence, and action that arrives after careful visual setup.

It should not be collapsed into generic Shaw Brothers studio style, modern wire-fu
inflation, or glossy contemporary wuxia trailer language. King Hu worked in and beyond
Shaw contexts; this profile is about a prompt/storyboard grammar, not a studio taxonomy.

Pollution boundary: do not use Shaw Brothers Clear Water Bay / Movietown backlot
theatricality as a King Hu proxy. Do not use saturated red-blue Shaw-style hard-light
spectacle as the default color logic. If a source script contains snow, mythic creatures,
or rescue melodrama, those are story constraints only; the original snow mountain /
qilin script is a story constraint, not King Hu evidence.

## Good Means

- spatial intelligence before action density: the viewer understands doors, windows, rafters, corridors, courtyards, bamboo, rocks, screens, and hiding places before the action erupts
- natural or architectural space carries the scene: ruined fort, inn, courtyard, temple, bamboo, desert edge, mountain path, or village threshold should shape blocking when the story permits
- delayed spectacle before action payoff: suspense, watching, listening, glances, and staged withholding carry value before combat begins
- Beijing opera rhythm shapes movement, percussion, eye-lines, gesture, and burst timing
- dance-like combat treats swordplay as choreographed motion through space, not only impact on bodies
- widescreen compositions full of graceful character movement carry relations between factions, witnesses, pursuers, monks, officials, and knight-errants
- inn / confined-space faction choreography turns tables, stairs, windows, thresholds, beams, curtains, and rooflines into tactical story elements
- female knight-errant figures carry moral steadiness, restraint, and competence without reducing them to decorative fantasy icons
- spiritual or philosophical pressure can enter through landscape, temple, silence, monks, empty space, or final unresolved stillness
- editing may use glimpses, sudden bursts, offscreen implication, and partial visibility rather than showing every move plainly
- action has ethical stakes: protection of the innocent, resistance to corrupt authority, loyalty, justice, or moral rectitude

## Bad Means

- generic AI wuxia trailer inflation adds flying shots, particle glow, slow-motion hero poses, and epic adjectives without spatial or ethical setup
- modern wire-fu inflation makes movement weightless spectacle without Beijing opera rhythm, tactical space, or delayed payoff
- modern xianxia gloss turns knight-errants into celestial romance figures with luminous magic and decorative robes
- Shaw-style backlot spectacle substitutes stone flats, red-blue theatrical lighting, and generic studio snow for King Hu spatial tension
- combat density overwhelms the setup, watching, hiding, listening, and factional tension that make the burst meaningful
- every panel becomes a beautiful action pose while ignoring doors, thresholds, architecture, terrain, eye-lines, and witnesses
- "King Hu" becomes a label for any vintage wuxia image instead of a specific rhythm of staging, cutting, and moral pressure
- female warriors are treated as costume spectacle rather than active moral/tactical agents
- spiritual imagery is used as vague mysticism instead of pressure, silence, irony, or philosophical turn

## Prompt Self-Audit Questions

`prompt_self_audit_questions`:

1. Does the prompt establish tactical space before action: entrances, exits, thresholds, heights, hiding places, witnesses, and lines of sight?
2. Does the prompt choose natural or architectural space rather than defaulting to Shaw-style studio backlot theatricality?
3. Does it create delayed spectacle, or does it rush directly into action coverage?
4. Does movement feel like Beijing opera rhythm / dance-like combat rather than generic wire-fu?
5. Are moral and political stakes visible enough to keep the action from becoming decorative?
6. If a female knight-errant appears, is she an agent with restraint, competence, and moral/tactical force?
7. Could the same prompt accidentally generate modern wire-fu inflation, modern xianxia gloss, generic Shaw studio style, or generic AI wuxia trailer inflation?
8. Is spiritual or philosophical pressure expressed through scene rhythm, silence, landscape, temple/monastic presence, or unresolved stillness rather than vague mystic glow?

## Image Self-Audit Questions

`image_self_audit_questions`:

1. Can the viewer read the space and infer where action could enter, exit, hide, or erupt?
2. Does the image show waiting, watching, listening, or tactical withholding, not only combat impact?
3. Do bodies move gracefully through widescreen space rather than posing as isolated hero icons?
4. Do architecture, inn space, bamboo, courtyard, temple, mountain path, desert edge, or landscape shape the drama?
5. Does the image avoid Shaw Brothers Clear Water Bay / Movietown backlot theatricality, saturated red-blue Shaw-style hard-light spectacle, modern wire-fu inflation, modern xianxia glow, generic AI wuxia trailer polish, and action-poster excess?
6. Does glimpse editing translate into partial visibility, occlusion, offscreen implication, or panel sequencing rather than a single overloaded still?
7. What one change would most improve King Hu alignment without inventing unsupported plot facts?

## Source Notes

`source_notes`:

- independent source notes: Harvard Film Archive frames King Hu as a major wuxia auteur whose mise-en-scene, movement, decor, art direction, and spatial composition express character relations.
- independent source notes: David Bordwell's Criterion essay emphasizes Hu's story delay, drawn shot planning, Beijing opera-derived combat, dance comparison, intricate sets/costumes, inn convergence, female warriors, widescreen compositions, glimpses, and daring cutting.
- independent source notes: Bordwell's later King Hu writing stresses slow buildup followed by percussive bursts, action rhythms recalling Beijing opera, staccato dance, moral rectitude, and long-shot spatial play.
- independent source notes: Senses of Cinema emphasizes inn films, political allegory, choreography of tavern space, Beijing opera rhythm, mythical aspects of movement, and the female knight-errant model.
- scope note: this profile is built for prompt/storyboard/image generation, not a complete film-historical account.
- adaptation note: glimpse editing must be translated into partial visibility, occlusion, offscreen implication, or panel sequencing because a still image cannot literally edit.
- contamination note: the original snow mountain / qilin script is a story constraint, not King Hu evidence.
